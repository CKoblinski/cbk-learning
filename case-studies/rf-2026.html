Case Study · Learning Design

Game-Based Onboarding That Teaches Finance Without Feeling Like Finance

We built Ramp Fundamentals — a story-driven course adopted org-wide for all new hires — that turned complex financial concepts into scenarios people actually engaged with. The engagement led to two separate projects.

The Challenge

How do you get hundreds of non-finance employees to actually understand finance?

Ramp is a finance automation platform. But not everyone at Ramp comes from a finance background — and they shouldn't have to. Engineers, designers, salespeople, and operations staff all needed to understand what finance teams actually do, how the product fits into their customers' world, and how to talk about it credibly.

The scope was wide: accounting fundamentals, balance sheets, income statements, cash flow, ERPs, ideal customer profiles, personas, and Ramp's competitive positioning. Traditional compliance-style training wasn't going to cut it — the course had to make these concepts stick for people who'd never studied finance.

01

Dense subject matter

Finance and accounting concepts that most employees have never studied — taught in a way that sticks without a textbook.

02

Diverse audience

Engineers, designers, salespeople, operations — all with different levels of financial literacy and very different day-to-day contexts.

03

Had to scale

This wasn't a workshop for 20 people. It needed to work for every new hire and be available on-demand across the entire organization.

The Approach

A business that grows with you — from farm stand to national chain

Instead of teaching finance concepts in isolation, we built a narrative. Learners follow Alex, an entrepreneur who starts a farm stand and grows it into a national grocery chain. Each stage of Alex's business introduces the financial concepts that stage demands — so the complexity scales naturally with the story.

Step 01

We started with the experts

We partnered with Dave Wieseneck, one of Ramp's experts in residence, and interviewed team leads across the organization. The goal: capture the knowledge that matters most from the people who know it best, then translate it into language anyone can follow.

Step 02

Finance concepts through a growing business

At the farm stand, learners meet balance sheets and cash accounting. When Alex opens a grocery store, they encounter income statements, general ledgers, and double-entry accounting. By the time the business goes national, it's cash flow management, ERPs, and financial controls. No concept is abstract — every one is tied to a decision Alex has to make.

Step 03

Customer scenarios built on a 5-act framework

For domain-specific knowledge, we designed a narrative framework that mirrors real customer journeys: Catalyst, Diagnosis, Exploration, Implementation, Resolution. Each scenario is driven by a different business trigger — a new CFO, rapid growth, a funding round — so learners practice navigating the situations they'll actually encounter with Ramp's customers.

Step 04

AI-generated imagery that feels real

We used custom AI-generated images throughout — licensing photos of real humans, then editing backgrounds to fit each scenario. The result: characters that look like actual coworkers in actual work situations, not generic stock photography or obvious AI artifacts. Every image served the story.

What We Built

A multi-part course spanning finance, product, and customer knowledge

The engagement covered two major projects across multiple lessons — from foundational finance literacy to customer-facing product expertise.

Finance & Accounting Foundations

A shared vocabulary around how finance teams operate — the core concepts every employee needs to understand to advocate for the product effectively.

Domain & Product Expertise

Advanced modules covering customer landscapes, industry verticals, and persona-driven communication strategies that help teams build credibility with finance leaders.

The Results

Measurable confidence gains — without a single push campaign

The course was adopted org-wide and included in onboarding for all new hires. These results came organically — no mandatory rollout, no major internal campaign. People took it because they wanted to.
