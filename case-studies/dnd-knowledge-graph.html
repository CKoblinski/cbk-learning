I run a four-year Dungeons & Dragons campaign. 115 sessions recorded on Zoom. If you pointed ChatGPT at one of those transcripts, it could summarize it. What it could not do is tell you why the silence in session 103 mattered, or that the NPC who just died was the mentor from session 47.

That's the gap most AI tools have. They are stateless. They forget every conversation the moment it ends. For a D&D campaign, that means bad recaps. For a business, it means your AI can't see the last three years of context that matters most.

This case study is about the other half of the pipeline. Not turning transcripts into video — turning them into memory.

Why This Goes First

If you skip name normalization, your graph ends up with ten different nodes for the same character. The AI confidently extracts relationships between "Hojbejrg" and "Hodge" as if they are two people. Every downstream query is corrupted.

The Table Is Half the Work

The correction table is not auto-generated. I built it by hand from four years of listening to my friends butcher fantasy names. Each entry gets injected into the extraction prompt as ground truth. When the AI sees "Anchor El" in a transcript, it knows that means Ank'Harel.

Business Parallel

Every company has a version of this problem. Product names. Customer names. Internal acronyms. Slack handles. If you want useful AI over your data, you need a canonical dictionary. It's unglamorous work. It's also the whole game.

Parallel Sub-Agents

I run five Claude Sonnet sub-agents simultaneously, each processing one session. Each session takes three to four minutes. Processing 20 sessions in a batch of five parallel agents takes under 20 minutes wall-clock. Serial execution would be over an hour.

Schema With Provenance

Every entity and edge carries three things: a confidence score (0 to 1), a source quote, and the session number it came from. Without provenance, you cannot verify the graph later. You also cannot distinguish things the AI was sure about from things it guessed.

Anti-Hallucination Rules

The extraction prompt has an explicit rule: if an entity is not mentioned by name in the transcript, use a descriptive ID like "char_unnamed_tech_elf." Do not invent proper nouns. This one rule catches more hallucinations than any QC layer.

The Insight

Every D&D session starts with a recap. That recap is the DM telling the players what was important about last week. It is the highest-quality signal for what matters in the prior session. So process session N+1 first. Extract its recap. Use that recap to tell the extractor "focus on these things" when you process session N.

How It Works in Code

After extraction, session N+1's dm_recap_summary is stored on the session node in the graph. When the prompt for session N is built, the pipeline looks for session N+1 in the graph, pulls its recap summary, and injects it into the prompt as priority guidance. Events mentioned in the recap get a confidence bump.

Business Parallel

Meeting notes have the same shape. Next week's meeting starts with "last week we decided X." That's a free, pre-existing, high-quality summary of what mattered. Most meeting-intelligence tools don't use it because they process chronologically. Reverse the order.

Why No AI in the QC Layer

Every rule that can be a rule should be a rule. Rules are free, fast, deterministic, and explainable. I only use AI where I can't write the rule. For ninety percent of graph QC, I can write the rule.

First-Meeting Detection

Merge knows which session an entity first appears in. When you click a node, the detail panel shows "First seen in Session 83." This metadata powers spoiler filtering if you want a player-safe version of the graph.

Business Parallel

Your CRM has duplicate contact records. Your knowledge base has the same article written four times by four different people. Entity resolution is unglamorous, cheap to automate, and worth every hour you spend on it. The rest of your AI pipeline only works if this layer does.

Why Subgraph Extraction Matters

Sending the full 1,067-node graph to the AI on every query would cost twenty times more per call and give you worse answers. Focused context beats bulk context. Extract the 1% of the graph that matters, send that.

GitHub Pages is static hosting. It cannot hold an API key. Without a proxy, the Anthropic key would be in the page source for anyone to grab. The Cloudflare Worker holds the key server-side, enforces rate limits (five per minute, fifty per day per IP, five hundred per day total), and locks CORS to the case study domain.

Philosophies

How I think about building AI systems that have memory

01

Context Is the Whole Game

Without context, AI gives you average answers. With context, AI gives you your answers. The context is already in your data. The work is making it queryable.

A salesperson doesn't need generic advice on closing. They need to know what this specific customer said in the call six months ago, what was promised by the last account manager, what was left unresolved. That information exists. It's in Slack, Gmail, Zoom recordings, CRM notes. But it's not accessible to any AI tool out of the box.

This project is one way to solve that. Extract the relationships. Build the graph. Point the AI at the subgraph that matters.

Context is not a prompt trick. It's a data layer you build once and query forever.
02

Structured Data Compounds. Summaries Don't.

"Tell me about Hoj" gives you a paragraph. A structured entity with 17 fields and 45 edges gives you something you can filter, cross-reference, aggregate, and re-query.

Summaries are terminal. Once you write one, the rest of the information is compressed away. A graph is generative. You can always ask it a new question because the underlying entities still have structure.

The rule I follow: if a piece of information might be relevant to more than one future question, store it structurally. Not as a summary.

A summary is a frozen answer to yesterday's question. A graph is a system that answers tomorrow's.
03

Confidence, Not Certainty

Every extraction has a confidence score. Every edge has a source quote. Every quote is tied to a session number. When you click an edge in the graph, you see where it came from.

This is how you make AI output trustworthy. Not by making the AI better. By making it show its work.

If you cannot audit the AI's reasoning trail, you can never safely act on its output. For anything that matters, provenance is more valuable than accuracy.

Build provenance into your data from day one. Retrofitting it later is almost impossible.
04

Build the Memory. Let AI Query It.

Most AI apps try to cram context into every prompt. Send the whole document. Send the whole conversation history. Send every possibly-relevant file. It's expensive, slow, and the model still loses track of what matters.

The alternative: put context in a graph. At query time, extract the small subgraph that actually matters for the specific question. Pass only that.

One of my queries uses about 2,000 tokens of context instead of the full 1.8MB graph. That's four orders of magnitude cheaper per call.

Cheap AI lives on top of expensive data work. Not the other way around.

Lessons Learned

What I'd do differently and why it matters

01

Order of Operations Matters More Than Pipeline Quality

I processed sessions in natural order (oldest first). Every extraction was blind to what came next. The quality was fine but every session was re-inventing context the DM had already summarized seven days later.

When I flipped to processing highest-first, each extraction was primed with the next session's recap as priority guidance. The confidence scores on extracted moments went up. The extractor stopped inventing plot points because I was telling it what the DM had confirmed mattered.

I didn't change the extractor. I changed the order.

Before you upgrade your model, check whether you're feeding it the right context in the right order.
02

The Most Common Extraction Error Has a Name

It's "enemy-as-ally." The extractor will confidently tag a hostile NPC as ALLIED_WITH a player character because they spoke politely in the scene. Happens in about five percent of sessions.

I wrote a six-line validator that flags any edge where an entity with subtype: enemy or subtype: creature has an ALLIED_WITH relationship with a PC. That one rule catches it every time.

The lesson isn't "AI is bad at this." The lesson is: you can predict the specific ways your AI pipeline will fail, and you can write deterministic checks for those specific failures. Do it once. Saves you every time.

Name the failure modes. Write the rules. Don't rely on the AI to catch itself.
03

Provenance Is Cheaper Than Trust

Early extractions had no source quotes. Everything looked clean until I tried to verify a specific edge and realized I had no way to check if it was real.

Retrofitting provenance meant re-running every extraction. Expensive. Painful. Avoidable.

Now every field carries: source quote, session number, confidence score. When the AI says "Eas has romantic tension with Bixie," I can click the edge and see the quote that produced it. Five seconds to verify instead of five minutes to re-read the transcript.

If your AI output is ever going to inform a decision, build the audit trail before you need it.
04

Parallelize Extraction, Serialize Merging

Five sub-agents extracting in parallel finish in three to four minutes instead of twenty. Huge wall-clock win.

But merging must be serial. Two extractions trying to write to the graph file at the same time produces corruption I spent a day debugging before I figured it out.

Concurrency where it's safe. Discipline where it isn't. Know the difference in your own pipeline.

Parallelism is a sharp tool. Use it where independent work happens. Don't use it where shared state changes.

Where This Goes at Work

D&D is the sandbox. Here's the shape of the problem for actual organizations.

Institutional Memory

Your company's last four years are in Slack. Your AI cannot see any of it.

Every decision your team has made is documented somewhere. The strategy doc from Q3 last year. The Slack thread where you decided to cut that feature. The customer call that made you pivot.

When a new hire asks "why do we do it this way," they get a shrug. When your AI assistant gets the same question, it makes something up.

Same pattern as D&D: extract decisions, participants, and outcomes into a graph. Ask it "why did we choose vendor X" and get a grounded answer with links to the actual thread.

Customer Intelligence

Your sales team's AI writes outreach emails like it just met the customer.

You've been selling to this company for eighteen months. Three account managers have talked to them. There's a folder of call recordings, a thread of email replies, a pile of Loom videos.

Your AI tool sees none of that. It writes a generic email.

Same pattern as D&D: a transcript of a customer call is the same shape as a D&D session. Extract the relationship graph. Next time someone writes outreach to that customer, the AI has actual context.

Learning & Development

Your instructors are designing next semester without access to last semester's lessons.

This is my world. A training team runs forty-plus learning experiences a year. They record debriefs, write retros, capture feedback. Six months later, that knowledge is in someone's head. If that person leaves, it's gone.

A knowledge graph over training artifacts lets instructors ask "what do we know about how software engineers respond to case-study format" and get grounded answers across years of prior work. Not summaries. Evidence.

Same pattern as D&D: session transcripts are training artifacts. Learner behavior is character behavior. The graph remembers what each cohort did so the next cohort's design is smarter.
