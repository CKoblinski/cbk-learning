Concept map

What we walked through.

The through-line, from the first slide to the last. Each card is a tap stop you can come back to.

01 · The shift

2023 was prompts. 2026 is judgment.

In 2023 the skill was instructing a tool. In 2026 the tool builds its own solution and the skill is judging a collaborator with expertise you don't fully share.

02 · How LLMs work

Plinko, not Excel.

Generative AI is probabilistic. Same prompt, different answers. That's why it's creative and why it hallucinates — same mechanism.

03 · Model types

Linear → Reasoning → Agentic.

Linear (Gemini Flash) answers fast. Reasoning models think before they speak. Agentic models call tools — search the web, read your Drive, write a Doc — without you babysitting each step.

04 · The jagged frontier

What AI can and can't do is jagged.

Some "hard" tasks AI nails. Some "easy" tasks it flops. Stop assuming, start testing — the frontier shifts with every model release.

05 · Where you live

Expertise × attention.

Start in "amplify my strengths" (high expertise, high attention). Stay out of "do it for me" (low expertise, low attention) — that's where AI mistakes become your mistakes.

06 · Cognitive offloading

Narrow good. Broad dangerous.

Handing off a step in a process is fine. Handing off the whole process means you stop building the skill to catch AI when it's wrong. Same lesson factory automation learned in the 80s.

07 · Difficulty

Germane vs. extraneous.

Germane difficulty is what makes the task worth doing — it builds you up. Extraneous difficulty is just friction. Automate the friction, keep the muscle-building.

08 · The 1979 rule

"A computer must never make a management decision."

IBM said it in 1979 and it still holds. A computer can't be held accountable, so it shouldn't be the one deciding. You stay accountable.

Heard in the room

Your team's actual use cases — with prompts to try.

These came up by name in our session. Pick one. Try the prompt this week. Bring what worked (and what didn't) to session two.

Ryan Operations

Scrape public databases for funders, surveys, partners.

You scraped 130 funder databases in 10 minutes with two prompts. Scale that to global-health household surveys, partner contact lists across countries, board-and-network mapping. Months of manual work, offloaded — but only the public layer.

Build a table of every foundation in [country] that has funded youth-employment programs in the last 5 years. Columns: name, total giving, grant size range, focus areas, last grant date, link to source. Only use publicly available sources and cite each row.
Sue Strategy

Synthesize five-plus strategic documents into one read.

The new strategic-planning cycle has surveys, retros, board notes, and the prior plan. Drop them all into one chat and have it weigh, conflict-check, and surface the through-line — not just summarize each.

I'm attaching five documents from our strategic-planning process. (1) Identify the three themes that appear in all five. (2) Flag any place two documents contradict each other. (3) List the top five recommendations weighted by how often they recur and how recent the document is.
Asia Programs

Real-time French ↔ English in 1:1s.

You and Ryan tested Gemini live-translating between French and English with similar vocal range. That's not document translation — that's a meeting tool. Worth testing with field teams whose first language isn't English.

For your follow-up: use Gemini Live's translation in a low-stakes 1:1 with a French-speaking teammate. Note (a) one moment it nailed nuance, (b) one moment it flattened it. Bring both to session two.
Garrett Volunteer support

Coach volunteer writing without rewriting it for them.

Your point: for volunteers whose English isn't first language, the win isn't grammar-fix-by-AI — it's "have you considered this perspective in your story?" Ask AI to coach the writer instead of replacing them.

Read this draft blog post by a volunteer. Don't rewrite it. Instead: (1) ask three questions that would make the story more specific. (2) Suggest one perspective they could add (the kid's view, the family's view, the community's view). (3) Flag any sentences where meaning is unclear — but let the writer fix them.
Virginia Communications

Translation that keeps your voice.

You translate constantly between languages and AI handles most of it. The next move is keeping your register — formal vs. warm, regional vs. neutral — instead of the AI's defaults.

Translate this English text into [target language]. Constraints: (1) keep the tone informal and warm — this is for community members, not government. (2) Use regional vocabulary where it exists. (3) After the translation, list three word choices a native speaker might disagree with and why you made them.

Guardrails (from Ryan's closing)

What you can and can't put in Gemini.

The single most important slide from this session. Your enterprise Gemini is bound by an agreement that protects your data — most other AI tools are not. Keep this rule of thumb close.

GREEN · safe to use

  • Drafts, brainstorms, outlines
  • Public reports, public data, public databases
  • Your own writing (for feedback)
  • Translation of non-personal text
  • Training reports without participant PII
  • Synthesis across CorpsAfrica's own program documents (in Gemini Enterprise)

YELLOW · be deliberate

  • Internal strategy docs (consider redacting names)
  • Country-specific impact data (think about re-identification risk)
  • Donor-facing drafts (don't let AI's voice replace yours)
  • First-name + DOB + country combos — they re-identify faster than you'd think
  • Anything that would embarrass the org if it surfaced in another chat

RED · never

  • Personnel files, job grading, salaries
  • Social security or tax ID numbers
  • Beneficiary PII (full name + location + ID)
  • Anything covered by an NDA
  • Free AI tools with any of the above (ChatGPT free trains on your data)
  • Final decisions that need human accountability — that's 1979 IBM's whole point

The 2×2

Where to spend your AI energy.

The map I use when someone says "should I automate this?" Two axes — how much you know about the task, and how much you actually care about it right now. Start top-right. Avoid bottom-left.
